\title{Generative Models for Cat Face Synthesis: \\ A Comparative Study of Variational Autoencoders, Generative Adversarial Networks, and Diffusion Models}

\author[affiliation={1}]{Pawel}{Pozorski}
\author[affiliation={1}]{Pawel}{Florek}

\address{
    $^1$ Warsaw University of Technology, Poland
}

\email{\{pawel.pozorski, pawel.florek\}.stud@pw.edu.pl}

\keywords{generative models, variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, diffusion models, PixelCNN, latent diffusion, image synthesis, cat generation, FID, hyperparameter optimization, mode collapse}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
We train and compare seven generative models on $128 \times 128$ cat faces: $\beta$-VAE and VQ-VAE-1; WGAN-GP and SN-GAN; pixel DDIM and a tiny latent diffusion model; and a PixelCNN prior over VQ codes. Each hyperparameter cell is run with three seeds and ranked by Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) where checkpoints exist; sample grids and latent interpolations cover the rest. WGAN-GP achieves the lowest FID ($174.4$), ahead of SN-GAN ($226.1$) and the VAE/VQ-prior baselines. On Apple MPS, tiny LDM samples about $5\times$ faster than PixelCNN through the same frozen VQ decoder, but those checkpoints decode to noise whereas PixelCNN keeps weak spatial structure. A chimera extension trains WGAN-GP on $\sim 25{,}000$ mixed dog--cat images at $64 \times 64$; outputs blend species features rather than separating classes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

VAEs \cite{kingma2013auto}, GANs \cite{NIPS2014_f033ed80}, and diffusion models \cite{NEURIPS2020_4c5bcfec} are the three standard approaches to image synthesis. They differ in training stability, sample sharpness, and inference cost---there is no single winner. We keep the data pipeline fixed: Kaggle cat faces cropped from \texttt{.cat} landmarks to $128 \times 128$, with the same logging, early stopping, and evaluation for all seven trainers.

We report FID on held-out validation faces, latent interpolations for $\beta$-VAE and WGAN-GP, a PixelCNN--tiny-LDM prior comparison on frozen VQ decoders, and a chimera extension on mixed dogs and cats at $64 \times 64$. WGAN-GP and SN-GAN replace a plain DCGAN to reduce mode collapse on this single-class dataset.

Training comprised $141$ jobs ($138$ cat-face grid runs plus three chimera seeds). MLflow records $217$ cat-face runs ($157$ finished, $60$ failed; Table~\ref{tab:mlflow_summary}); chimera checkpoints are stored separately under \texttt{checkpoints/chimera/}. Rankings and sample analysis follow in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}

The primary dataset consists of over 9,000 cat images from the Kaggle Cat Dataset \cite{crawford2018cat}. Source files differ widely in resolution, aspect ratio, and head pose, so preprocessing is needed for comparable inputs. We standardize inputs as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Annotation Parsing}: Instead of relying on pre-trained face detectors, we extract the exact 9 coordinate points (eyes, mouth, ears) provided in the corresponding \texttt{.cat} annotation files.
    \item \textbf{Face Cropping}: Bounding boxes are dynamically calculated based on the extreme coordinates of the facial landmarks with a $35\%$ pad on the landmark extent (\texttt{BBOX\_PAD\_RATIO}$=0.35$).
    \item \textbf{Resizing}: Cropped regions are resized to $128 \times 128$ with Lanczos resampling (\texttt{PIL.Image.LANCZOS}).
    \item \textbf{Normalization}: All cat-face training tensors are mapped to $[-1, 1]$ via \texttt{ToTensor} and per-channel normalization with mean $0.5$ and std $0.5$, matching $\tanh$ decoder/generator outputs; pixel DDIM sampling clamps predicted images to $[-1, 1]$ each step.
    \item \textbf{Training augmentation}: The cat-face train split applies \texttt{RandomHorizontalFlip} ($p{=}0.5$); validation tensors are not augmented.
\end{enumerate}

Processed images are written to \texttt{train.npy} and \texttt{val.npy} using a $90/10$ train/validation split with a fixed assignment seed ($42$).

Comparison of raw vs. processed images is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:preprocessing_comparison}.

\subsection{Generative Model Architectures}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.25em, labelsep=0.5em, itemsep=0.6em, topsep=0.4em]
\item[Variational Autoencoders (VAE)]
VAEs define a probabilistic generative model via a learned latent variable distribution $q_\phi(z|x)$ parameterized by an encoder network. The generative process is specified by a decoder $p_\theta(x|z)$ where $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. We evaluate two VAE variants:

\textbf{$\beta$-VAE \cite{higgins2017betavae}:} A standard VAE with convolutional encoder/decoder and a $\beta$ parameter that controls the trade-off between reconstruction accuracy and prior matching. Grid search varies: $\text{latent\_dim} \in \{64, 128\}$, $\beta \in \{1.0, 4.0\}$.

\textbf{VQ-VAE-1:} A discrete VAE using vector quantization in the latent space \cite{NIPS2017_7a98af17}. Instead of a continuous distribution, VQ-VAE discretizes the latent space into a learned codebook to reduce the blurriness typical of standard VAEs. Grid search varies: $\text{codebook\_size} \in \{512, 1024\}$, $\text{feat\_map\_size} \in \{16 \times 16, 8 \times 8\}$, and reconstruction loss (L1 vs.\ MSE). Non-swept defaults are $\text{embedding\_dim}=64$ and $\text{commitment\_cost}=0.25$.

\item[Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)]
GANs train a generator $G$ against a discriminator $D$. We use two stabilized variants instead of a plain DCGAN:

\textbf{WGAN-GP:} Wasserstein GAN \cite{pmlr-v70-arjovsky17a} with gradient penalty \cite{NIPS2017_892c3b1c}. Both GAN generators use latent dimension $128$. Grid search varies: $\text{n\_critic} \in \{3, 5\}$, batch size $\in \{64, 128\}$, learning rate schedule $\in \{\text{symmetric}, \text{TTUR}\}$.

\textbf{SN-GAN:} Spectral Normalization GAN \cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1802-05957} stabilizes discriminator training by normalizing weights with their largest singular value. Training uses one discriminator update per generator step and hinge losses. Grid search varies: batch size $\in \{64, 128\}$, learning rate schedule $\in \{\text{symmetric}, \text{TTUR}\}$, horizontal flip augmentation.

\item[Diffusion Models]
Diffusion models learn to reverse a noise-corruption process. Full-resolution pixel diffusion is slow, so we train two lighter setups:

\textbf{DDIM:} A faster sampling approach for diffusion models \cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2010-02502} that uses a deterministic sampling procedure, enabling inference in fewer steps. A U-Net denoises $128 \times 128$ pixel images with an $8 \times 8$ bottleneck over $T{=}1000$ forward diffusion steps; an exponential moving average (EMA) copy of the U-Net is used for validation and sampling. Grid search varies: noise schedule $\in \{\text{linear}, \text{cosine}\}$, base channels $\in \{64, 128\}$, and DDIM steps $\in \{50, 100\}$ (stored in each checkpoint and used at generation time).

\textbf{Tiny Latent Diffusion Model (LDM):} Following Rombach et al. \cite{Rombach_2022_CVPR}, we train a lightweight U-Net diffusion model on \emph{continuous} pre-quantization latents from a frozen VQ-VAE-1 encoder; vector quantization is applied only at decode time. Latent activations are scaled to unit variance using validation-set statistics before diffusion training. The VQ encoder/decoder weights are not updated during LDM training; only the U-Net (and its EMA copy) is optimized. DDIM sampling produces a latent tensor that is unscaled, quantized, and passed through the frozen decoder. The frozen VQ-VAE per seed is chosen from the VQ grid sweep as the checkpoint with lowest validation reconstruction loss (\texttt{prior\_best\_by\_seed.json}). Grid search matches DDIM: schedule $\in \{\text{linear}, \text{cosine}\}$, base channels $\in \{64, 128\}$, DDIM steps $\in \{50, 100\}$.

\item[PixelCNN Prior over VQ Codes (Baseline)]
VQ-VAE-1 alone defines $p(x|z)$ via the decoder but not a prior $p(z)$ over discrete code indices. For unconditional synthesis we therefore compare two learned priors on the same frozen VQ-VAE:

\textbf{PixelCNN \cite{oord2016pixel}:} A lightweight autoregressive model with masked convolutions predicts codebook indices on the $16 \times 16$ or $8 \times 8$ latent grid (depending on the VQ-VAE configuration). Architecture is fixed at 128 hidden channels and 10 masked layers (not swept). After the VQ-VAE grid sweep, PixelCNN is trained once per seed on the manifest-selected frozen VQ-VAE; a follow-up \texttt{pixelcnn-experiment} script also trains a single Tiny LDM configuration per seed (EMA on, DDIM steps $=100$) for wall-clock and qualitative comparison against PixelCNN.
\end{description}

\subsection{Training Setup and Hyperparameter Grid}

All models employ the Adam optimizer \cite{kingma2014adam} with $\text{lr} = 2 \times 10^{-4}$ and $(\beta_1, \beta_2) = (0.5, 0.999)$ for VAE, diffusion, and PixelCNN trainers. GAN generators use $(\beta_1, \beta_2) = (0.5, 0.999)$ under WGAN-GP and $(0.0, 0.9)$ under SN-GAN. Training uses a high epoch cap ($1000$) with early stopping (patience $= 15$, $\text{min\_epochs} = 20$, $\text{min\_delta} = 10^{-4}$). $\beta$-VAE and VQ-VAE early stopping monitor \textbf{reconstruction loss} rather than total loss (which includes KL or VQ terms that can plateau early). PixelCNN and diffusion trainers monitor validation loss; GAN early stopping monitors generator loss (\texttt{g\_loss}). SN-GAN horizontal-flip augmentation applies only to real images fed to the discriminator when enabled in the grid. All main experiments use Apple MPS unless overridden; metrics and sample grids are logged to MLflow.

Symmetric vs.\ TTUR learning-rate schedules \cite{heusel2017gans} are implemented by pairing generator learning rate $2 \times 10^{-4}$ with discriminator learning rate $\in \{2 \times 10^{-4}, 4 \times 10^{-4}\}$.

Each hyperparameter configuration is trained independently across three fixed random seeds $\{42, 0, 3407\}$. Seeds are reset in PyTorch, NumPy, and Python before every run. Grid-sweep results are compared per cell; evaluation metrics are aggregated over the same seed list for reproducibility. Table~\ref{tab:hyperparam_grid} lists the search space per family; the \textbf{Runs} column counts trained jobs as (grid cells) $\times$ 3 seeds. The main cat-face grid comprises $132$ such jobs ($44$ hyperparameter cells across six families). Adding three fixed-architecture PixelCNN prior trainings and three fixed Tiny LDM prior trainings (from \texttt{pixelcnn-experiment}) yields $138$ cat-face training jobs. The chimera extension adds three further WGAN-GP runs at $64 \times 64$ ($141$ training jobs in total). MLflow may log more than one run record per planned job when sweeps are restarted; $217$ cat-face run records appear in the tracking database (Table~\ref{tab:mlflow_summary}), while chimera runs are stored under \texttt{checkpoints/chimera/}.

\subsection{Compute and Resource Strategy}

Training seven families on laptop-class hardware (Apple MPS) required fixing resolution, run length, and search breadth up front. All cat-face models train on $128 \times 128$ tensors stored as \texttt{.npy} shards so batches never reload full-resolution JPEGs. Early stopping (patience $15$, cap $1000$ epochs) cuts unpromising runs short. Hyperparameters are explored on a fixed grid so every job stays reproducible. Diffusion stays practical via DDIM sampling ($50$--$100$ steps at inference, not $1000$) and a tiny LDM on $16 \times 16$ VQ latents instead of pixel-space U-Nets at higher resolution. The chimera extension uses $64 \times 64$ center crops on $\sim 25{,}000$ images because landmark cropping is unavailable and $128 \times 128$ GAN training on the full mixed set was outside our time budget. MLflow logs validation metrics and $4 \times 4$ sample grids so we could monitor runs without keeping every process on the GPU.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.25em, labelsep=0.5em, itemsep=0.6em, topsep=0.4em]
\item[Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)]
FID \cite{heusel2017gans} compares the distribution of real validation images to unconditionally generated samples in the feature space of a pretrained Inception-v3 network. Lower FID indicates better alignment with the data distribution. We use each model's native sampling procedure (e.g., $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ for $\beta$-VAE and GANs, DDIM for pixel diffusion, random codes for VQ-VAE when no separate prior is evaluated). Generated tensors in $[-1,1]$ are denormalized to $[0,1]$, resized to $299 \times 299$, and passed through Inception-v3 with ImageNet input normalization before feature extraction.

We report FID on the held-out validation set for $\beta$-VAE, VQ-VAE-1, PixelCNN, WGAN-GP, and SN-GAN using $N{=}1000$ samples per checkpoint. DDIM and tiny LDM checkpoints from the main sweep were trained and logged to MLflow but are absent from the final \texttt{fid\_scores.json} export; we judge them from sample grids in Section~\ref{sec:results}. For each FID-evaluated family we score every hyperparameter grid cell that has a \texttt{best} checkpoint: FID is computed per seed, then mean $\pm$ standard deviation is reported per cell. Many cells were evaluated on a single seed ($87$ per-seed scores across $12$--$24$ cells per family). For VQ-linked models, frozen VQ weights per seed come from \texttt{prior\_best\_by\_seed.json}. PixelCNN and tiny LDM use their learned priors with that decoder; VQ-VAE FID uses random code indices as a weak baseline only.

\item[Latent Interpolation Analysis]
For models with a continuous latent vector $z \in \mathbb{R}^d$, we test whether the learned representation is semantically smooth. We sample two endpoints $z_1, z_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ from the prior used at generation time, form eight uniformly spaced convex combinations
$z_\alpha = (1-\alpha) z_1 + \alpha z_2$, $\alpha \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{9}, \ldots, 1\}$,
and decode each $z_\alpha$ to an image, yielding a strip of ten frames. Continuity of appearance along the strip is judged qualitatively. This protocol is applied to $\beta$-VAE and WGAN-GP using the \texttt{best\_run} hyperparameter cell from \texttt{fid\_scores.json} (lowest mean FID); it is not used for discrete VQ codes or diffusion latents without an explicit interpolation definition.

\item[PixelCNN vs.\ Tiny LDM Prior Comparison]
Using the same frozen VQ-VAE per seed, we compare autoregressive PixelCNN sampling against Tiny LDM (DDIM steps $=100$, EMA) on wall-clock generation time and side-by-side sample grids. Timings use device synchronization on Apple MPS; outputs are written to \texttt{results/prior\_comparison/}. Training durations for all families are summarized separately from MLflow run timestamps (Table~\ref{tab:mlflow_summary}).

\item[Exploratory Extension: Mixed Distributions (Chimera)]
We train WGAN-GP on cats and dogs together at $64 \times 64$ ($\sim 25{,}000$ images, 90/10 train/val split per seed) to test whether an unconditional generator separates the classes or averages them into hybrid faces. We skipped diffusion (too slow at this scale) and VAEs (blur would hide the effect). Early stopping matches the cat study but caps at $100$ epochs.
\end{description}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Fr\'echet Inception Distance}

Table~\ref{tab:fid_best} and Figure~\ref{fig:fid_best_runs} summarize the lowest-mean-FID cell per family. WGAN-GP leads; GANs score clearly below the VAE and VQ-prior baselines in this export.

The WGAN-GP grid spans FID $174.4$--$287.9$ over $24$ cells. Its best cell uses $n_{\text{critic}}{=}5$, batch $128$, and symmetric rates ($\text{lr}_G{=}\text{lr}_D{=}2{\times}10^{-4}$). Worst cells paired batch $64$ with TTUR ($\text{lr}_D{=}4{\times}10^{-4}$). SN-GAN peaked at batch $128$, symmetric rates, and \emph{no} horizontal flip ($226.1$); flip augmentation pushed some cells above $370$. For $\beta$-VAE, $\beta{=}1.0$ and $\text{latent\_dim}{=}128$ beat $\beta{=}4.0$ (best $278.3$ vs.\ worst $335.6$). VQ-VAE-1 preferred an $8{\times}8$ map, $K{=}512$, and MSE ($243.5$); $16{\times}16$ maps with L1 scored worse. VQ-VAE FID uses random code indices---a decoder sanity check, not a learned prior. DDIM and tiny LDM runs from the main sweep are missing from \texttt{fid\_scores.json}; we judge them from sample grids below.

WGAN-GP also has the tightest FID spread across grid cells (Figure~\ref{fig:fid_grid}). \section{Conclusions}

Stabilized GANs ranked highest on FID for $128 \times 128$ cat faces; within WGAN-GP alone, hyperparameters moved scores by more than $110$ points. VAE outputs stayed blurry despite reasonable reconstruction, and neither VQ random codes nor PixelCNN matched GAN sample quality. Diffusion checkpoints trained successfully but were omitted from the FID export; qualitatively, pixel DDIM looked grainy and the fixed tiny-LDM prior decoded to noise.

PixelCNN sampled about $5\times$ slower than tiny LDM through the same decoder and still failed to produce recognizable cats. On mixed dog--cat data, the chimera WGAN-GP blended species rather than separating them; class labels would be needed for controlled multi-class generation.

VQ-VAE had the highest training failure rate ($27/57$ runs), SN-GAN horizontal flip often hurt FID, and validation metrics only loosely predicted FID across grid cells. Limitations include incomplete per-seed FID coverage, missing diffusion FIDs, a single saved interpolation seed, and MPS-specific timings. Next steps: export diffusion FIDs, score all three seeds per grid cell, and try class-conditional training on mixed data.
